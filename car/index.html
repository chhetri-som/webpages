The Group B regulations fostered some of the fastest, most powerful, and most sophisticated rally cars ever built, and their era is commonly referred to as the golden era of rallying. However, a series of major accidents, some fatal, were believed to be caused by their outright speed. There was also a major lack of crowd control at events. After the death of Henri Toivonen and his co-driver Sergio Cresto in the 1986 Tour de Corse, the FIA banned the group from competing in the WRC from the following season, dropped its prior plans to introduce Group S, and designated Group A

New FISA groups

In 1982, the Fédération Internationale du Sport Automobile (FISA) restructured the production car category into three new groups - Group 1, Group 2 & Group A.
These cars had to have four seats. Homologation requirement was 5,000 and later reduced to 2,500

Group Overview

The Group B class was introduced for the 1982 World Rally Championship (WRC). This class allowed teams to build cars however they wanted, with very few restrictions and required only a total production of 200 homologation cars.

Place in rally

Group B was later redefined as sports GT cars in 1986. It combined and replaced Group III, Group IV and the production-derived Group V silhouette cars used in circuit racing.
Group V cars had never been permitted in the WRC for Manufacturers.
